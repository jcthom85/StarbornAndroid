# World 1: The Mines — Story Bible

**Status:** Canon (Week 1 Monday)
**Setting:** Stellarium Colony 4 — A Dominion-owned mining operation built into canyon walls on a sealed planet
**Tone:** Oppressive normalcy. The horror isn't monsters — it's paperwork, quotas, and the quiet knowledge that you're replaceable.
**Act:** Act 1 — The Spark

---

## I. World Identity

### What This World Is About

World 1 is a cage disguised as a job. Stellarium Colony 4 is a company town where the Dominion owns the rock, the air, the light schedule, and the people. Nobody calls it a prison because prisons don't pay scrip. The miners call it "the Hole" and they're not wrong — the entire colony is built underground, carved into canyon walls, with no natural light and recycled air that tastes like someone else already breathed it.

The player should feel the weight. Low ceilings. Narrow corridors. The constant hum of industrial machinery that you stop noticing after the first hour and can't sleep without after the first week. This is not a dungeon to be conquered — it's a life to be escaped.

### Visual Identity

- **Style Baseline:** World 1 is oppressive in story, but not visually joyless. It should match Starborn's chibi/anime sprites and comic-storybook backgrounds: bold silhouettes, thick-outline forms, chunky machinery, saturated accents, and clear readable landmarks.
- **Palette:** Rust orange, industrial yellow, gun-metal grey, emergency red, teal/cyan glow, and dirty black. Blue is mostly Dominion branding/system light, but the overall image language can still be colorful and punchy.
- **Lighting:** Sodium-vapor amber in residential areas. Harsh fluorescent white in Dominion zones. Red emergency strobes during lockdown. Use bright pools of color and glowing signage so scenes stay graphic and readable.
- **Architecture:** Exposed pipes, bolted panels, cable bundles, condensation on walls. Homestead feels jury-rigged, human, and scrappy. Logistics feels stamped-out and corporate. Both should remain stylized enough to sit beside cute character portraits and exaggerated enemy sprites.
- **Contrast:** The Ancient Chamber (Sector 4) breaks the visual language entirely — geometry that's too clean, angles too intentional, surfaces that don't rust. Dominion bolted warning stripes onto it, but they don't belong.
- **Do Not Overcorrect:** The colony is a company-town cage, but the art should not become grey military realism. It is a colorful anime/comic adventure world with labor horror underneath.

### Audio Identity

- **Homestead ambient:** Drill vibration through walls. Distant hydraulic clanks. The PA system cycling through productivity slogans ("Your Shift Is Your Contribution"). Ventilation that wheezes.
- **Dominion zones:** Quieter. The hum of servers. Synthetic chimes at checkpoints. The PA relay voice (Vale, though the player doesn't know it yet) is calm and measured.
- **The Source (first contact):** When the Tuning Fork activates, all ambient sound drops out. Replaced by a pressure — felt in the teeth and sternum before the ears register it. Not a melody. A single sustained tone, like the planet exhaling for the first time.

### The Colony's Social Landscape

Miners know the system is rigged. They're not naive. But resistance is expensive and the last group that tried (referenced in SQ04's hacked terminal: "If you're reading this, we failed") got recycled. Most people cope through small kindnesses (Sam & Ellie feeding broke workers), petty hustles (Scrapper's black market), and gallows humor. Nobody talks about "freedom" because the word doesn't fit down here. They talk about "getting by."

## II. Thematic Pillars

### 1. Ownership

Everything in the colony belongs to the Dominion — including the people. Nova is an "Asset." Her labor is a "Contribution." Her death would be "Recycling." The language strips humanity from every transaction. World 1 establishes that the enemy isn't a monster — it's a spreadsheet.

### 2. The Hustle

Nova survives by cheating. She rigs her mining cutter, trades on the black market, and takes shortcuts. This is her strength AND her flaw. The hustle is what gets her flagged for recycling (the power surge from the rigged cutter). The hustle is also what puts her in Sector 4 (Bogs sends her as punishment for the surge). Her greatest survival skill is the thing that keeps almost killing her.

### 3. The Cost of Caring

Jed cares about Nova, and it kills him. Zeke cares about one stranger, and it destroys his career. World 1 argues that caring is dangerous in a system designed to isolate people — but it's also the only thing that makes the system breakable.

### 4. The First Sound

The Source is introduced not as power, not as magic, but as *hearing something for the first time*. The Tuning Fork doesn't give Nova abilities — it shows her that the silence she's been living in was artificial. The noise of the mine was masking something underneath. This is the cosmological version of the colony's social lie: the system says "this is all there is," and the Source says "no, it isn't."

## III. Story Skeleton — Entry & Exit Beats

This is the structural frame for the **main quest line**. The entry and exit beats define where the plot starts and where it lands — they're the goalposts for main quests and key story events. Side quests, NPCs, shops, and environmental details don't need to push the plot forward. They have their own jobs: building the world, teaching mechanics, creating texture, and giving the player reasons to care about the place they're about to lose. Content should serve *something* — the plot, the world, a character, a mechanic — but not everything needs to serve the same thing.

## IV. Complete Narrative Throughline

### Prologue: The Signal (Cold Open)

Twenty years before the game begins. An Aethel-Dominion joint facility in Sector 9. Clean lab lighting. Fresh steel bolted onto ancient stone. Dominion warning stripes over Architect geometry.

Dominion consoles spit graphs and warnings: the Source is spiking. Red strobes. A siren chewing through the air. **"SOURCE BEAST CONTAINMENT BREACH."**

Orion rips a Chime from its cradle — a Source resonator keyed to his signature. "Get the signal out. If Veyra can hear me, it can find me." He slams the Chime into the launch tube and hits the release. It rockets away into darkness.

For a single impossible beat, the Chorus answers — not a voice, but a soft chord like many notes agreeing to carry it.

Orion dives into stasis. "Mute my signal. Now." The glass seals. A shadow lunges at the pod — cut to black.

**Function:** Establishes the Source as dangerous and real. Creates mystery (who was that? what was that creature?). The Chime's journey — from Orion's hand, to salvage, to Jed's scrap pile, to Nova's escape pod — is the invisible thread connecting World 1 to World 2.

### Hub 1: Homestead Quarter — "The Status Quo"

#### MQ01: Wake Up Call

Nova wakes in her bunk. The PA announces the shift. She reports to Jed's Workshop, where he's tinkering with a salvaged part.

Jed teaches basic tinkering (crafting tutorial). He warns Nova about her rigged cutter: "You keep pushing that thing and it'll push back." She dismisses him — quota is quota. They argue, not about safety, but about strategy: Jed says keep your head down and wait. Nova says waiting is dying slow.

*Emotional beat:* Warmth. Safety. The only time in the game Nova has a home and a parent in the same room.
*Action beat:* Jed grumbles about her risk-taking but quietly slides her a patched glove or a saved ration bar. A small, wordless kindness that proves he's not just a mentor, but a parent.

*What the player should notice:* Dominion propaganda posters ("Your Shift Is Your Contribution"). A "Days Since Last Accident" counter stuck at 0. A memorial wall for "retired assets" that nobody looks at directly. The bones of the system are visible to anyone paying attention.

*Player learns:* Movement, interaction, tinkering basics, the bunk-to-workshop route that establishes "home."

#### EVT_W1_00: The Volunteer (Early Hub 1 Exploration)

Zeke approaches Nova near the bulletin board, trying to recruit her for the "Volunteer Compliance Committee." He's awkward, earnest, and hiding behind his clipboard. Nova brushes him off. He watches her leave — impressed, not offended.

*Function:* Plants Zeke as a character before his MQ02 role. Establishes his voice (corporate buzzwords as coping mechanism). Creates a callback for MQ02 ("Oh, it's the volunteer again").

#### Side Quest Window (Hub 1)

After MQ01, the Homestead opens for exploration. Three side quests are available:

**SQ01: The Scavenger's Stash** (Scrapper)
Track a hidden explosives cache from a failed rebel operation. Scrapper knows where they hid it but is too paranoid to go himself.
- *Narrative hook:* The cache is old. The rebels who hid it are long recycled. Their names are scratched into the crate lid — the first evidence that resistance existed here and was crushed. The player finds a weapon, but the real discovery is a history of defiance that didn't survive.
- *Reward:* Pulse Grenade.

**SQ02: System Flush** (Doc)
Toxic fumes are backing up through the med-bay vents because Dominion won't pay for maintenance. Clear the blockage.
- *Narrative hook:* Doc reveals that "maintenance request denied" notices pile up faster than patients. The colony is rotting from the inside out. Dominion doesn't fix what it can replace.
- *Reward:* Weapon Mod: Corrosive Rounds.

**SQ03: Heavy Lifting** (Foreman Bogs) — MANDATORY / STRONGLY SIGNPOSTED
A cargo collapse is blocking the loading dock. Use the loader mech to clear debris.
- *Narrative hook:* Bogs doesn't care about the workers trapped behind the debris — he cares about the shipment schedule. Nova uses the mech to save the workers AND clear the dock. This is the first time she helps someone when she could've just done the job and left.
- *Reward:* Nova Skill: Hydraulic Kick (Guard Break/Stagger tutorial).
- *Design note:* Guard Break/Stagger is essential knowledge before the Warden. This quest must be completed (or very heavily signposted) before the player crosses into Hub 2.

#### MQ02: Paperwork

Nova needs mine clearance. She goes to the Admin Gate checkpoint. The guard won't let her through without a badge. She navigates the bureaucracy to the Admin Window.

Zeke is behind the glass. He recognizes her ("The volunteer!"). He pulls up her file to process the badge — and stops. Her file is flagged: **MANDATORY RETIREMENT. ASSET LIABILITY > PROJECTED VALUE. RECYCLE.**

The rigged cutter's power surge got her flagged. The system has already decided she's worth more dead than alive. Zeke stares at the "Confirm" button.

**Mini-Game: Paperwork Hacking.** Instead of a Hollywood cyber-hack, Nova (or the player via Zeke's hesitation) must select the correct bureaucratic lies to spoof the form. "Grid Instability" vs "Operator Error." It's not code-breaking; it's form-filling to save a life.

He overrides the flag. Slides the badge under the glass with a shaking hand.

*The Cost:* As the badge prints, a camera on the wall swivels to focus directly on Zeke. A silent "LOGGED" notification flashes on his console. He sees it. He knows he's caught. He gives Nova the badge anyway.

*Emotional beat:* The first crack in the system. Not a revolution — one scared clerk choosing not to kill one person. This is Zeke's inciting incident: the moment his lie ("rules keep me safe") fails him.

*What the player learns about the world:* People get recycled routinely. Zeke has processed these orders before without looking at the names. The banality of evil is administrative.

*Reward:* Mine Access Badge.
*Transition:* Admin Concourse (Public Border Zone) → Checkpoint (Restricted) → Hub 2: Logistics Sector.

### Hub 2: Logistics Sector — "The Descent"

#### MQ03: The Echo

Nova crosses into the Logistics Sector — sterile, bureaucratic, and heavily guarded. Foreman Bogs intercepts her. He knows about the surge. "You broke my breaker? Go to Sector 4 and clear the magnetic interference."

*Quest Logic:*
1. Initial Objective: "Clear the magnetic interference in Sector 4."
2. Mid-Quest Update (after weird geometry): "Investigate the unknown signal."
3. Post-Sync: "Escape with the Relic."

Nova descends via the Deep Elevator into the Old Tunnels. First full combat gauntlet. Rock-Borers burrow and ambush. Security Drones shoot low-damage lasers and call for backup if not killed quickly. The first Riot Shield encounter teaches Guard Break/Stagger — everything Hub 1's SQ03 prepared her for.

The tunnels get older and stranger as she descends. The walls stop looking carved and start looking *shaped*. The geometry changes. Angles that aren't human. Surfaces that don't rust. Dominion bolted warning stripes onto them, but the stripes look wrong — new paint on old bones. **(Architect Reveal #1: Dominion didn't build this. They found it.)**

At the center of the Ancient Chamber: the Tuning Fork. Humming. The "magnetic interference" that Bogs sent her to fix isn't interference at all — it's a signal Dominion's sensors can't decode.

Nova tries to clear it. Touches it. Syncs.

**EVT_W1_01: The Discovery**

The mine goes silent. All ambient sound drops out. The hum isn't sound — it's pressure, felt in the teeth and sternum before the ears register it. The universe stops holding its breath.

For one impossible beat, Nova isn't standing in the room. She IS the room. The rock. The air. The relic. Then the connection snaps.

She wakes on the floor, shivering, nosebleeding. Her head splits. For a beat, even Jed's name won't come — then it snaps back.

Somewhere far above, a Dominion sensor reads the spike and quietly screams.

*What the player learns:* The Source is real, it's physical, and it hurts. Power has a cost — she lost a sliver of memory (Jed's name, for just a second). This seeds the finale's ultimate cost (Nova permanently loses her connection to the Source and pieces of herself).

*Reward:* Echo Relic: The Tuning Fork. Source Art: Blast Wave (Nova).

#### SQ04: Protocol Override (Hub 2 — Server Room)

A hacked terminal left by a previous rebel group. Their code still works. "If you're reading this, we failed. But the code still works."

- *Narrative hook:* Hope outlasts the people who planted it. Another echo of the colony's buried history of resistance.
- *Reward:* Passive: Admin Access (hacking efficiency up).

#### SQ05: The Lost Shift (Hub 2 — Deep Mine)

A dead miner's datapad in a collapsed tunnel. A letter to family back on World 3: "They aren't paying us anymore. They're just keeping us."

- *Narrative hook:* The quiet horror of realizing the employment contract was always a cage. This miner knew it. He wrote it down. Nobody came.
- *Reward:* Weapon Mod: Recoil Dampener.

### The Lockdown — "No Going Back"

#### EVT_W1_02: The Lockdown

The Relic trips the alarm. Every screen in the sector turns red. The Warden appears on holographic displays:

"Security Breach in Sector 4. Biological contaminant detected. Initiate containment protocol. Purge all organic assets."

Then — a flicker. The red shifts to sterile blue for one beat. A different voice on the PA, calm and measured:

"Correction, Warden. Cruelty is inefficient. Simply recycle the asset."

The Warden's visor twitches, visibly irritated. The screen glitches back to red. "...Seize the assets. Alive."

*Function:* Two villain voices in one scene. The Warden is the immediate threat — loud, physical, present. The PA voice (Vale via orbital relay, though the player doesn't know this yet) is the structural threat — calm, distant, embedded in the walls. On first play, Vale sounds like a moderating force. On replay, you realize he wasn't saying "don't kill her." He was saying "don't waste resources." He's worse than the Warden because he's right about the math.

#### MQ04: Red Alert

The Logistics Sector goes hostile. Guards, drones, and automated defenses activate. Zeke contacts Nova via comms — he's in the server room, guiding her through maintenance tunnels.

Chase/gauntlet sequence. Nova fights through tunnels, crawls through vents, runs from closing blast doors. Zeke calls out guard positions and unlocks doors remotely. This is the first time they work as a team, even though they're separated by walls and floors.

*Emotional beat:* The colony Nova knew is gone. There's no going back to the bunk. No more shifts. No more "getting by." The system isn't ignoring her anymore — it's hunting her.

#### EVT_W1_03: The Sacrifice

Nova reaches the Cargo Lift. The controls are jammed. Warden's Enforcers breach the door behind her.

Jed appears — he followed her into the tunnels. He runs back to the manual override panel.

"Get out of here, Nova! Find the sky!"

He presses a humming Chime into her hand: "Found it in scrap. Dominion called it a Ghost Signal Cell. If you find a ship, this wakes it."

Jed smashes the panel. The lift door slams shut between them. Through the window, Nova sees Jed charge the Enforcers with his wrench as the lift rockets upward.

*Emotional climax of World 1.* The safety net is cut. Nova's mentor, the person who taught her everything she knows, chose to die so she could run. This is the moment her lie ("survival is enough") starts cracking — because Jed didn't survive. He *chose*. His last words weren't "be safe." They were "find the sky."

*What the player should feel:* Grief that burns fast and turns into forward momentum. There's no time to mourn. The lift is moving. The escape isn't over.

*Reward:* Chime (Ghost Signal Cell). Launch Bay path opens.

---

### The Escape — "The Launch"

#### MQ05: The Launch

Nova fights through the final stretch of Logistics to the Launch Bay. Security Checkpoint B. The pod bay. The Warden is waiting.

**Boss: The Warden**
The Warden is a wall of power armor and corporate authority.
- *Riot Shield:* Immune while guarding. Must be Guard Broken (Slide Kick, Hydraulic Kick, or Blast Wave) before the party can meaningfully damage him.
- *Summon Drone:* Calls 2 Scout Drones every 3 turns.
- *Gas Grenade:* AoE Burn DoT zone.

This fight tests everything Hub 1 and Hub 2 taught: Guard Break (Hydraulic Kick / Slide Kick), AoE clear (Pulse Grenade for drones), and Source power (Blast Wave to crack the shield).

Mid-fight, the PA cuts in again: "Pause. Secure the asset for recycling." The Warden hesitates, annoyed, then charges harder. **(Vale's second appearance. Two bosses in one fight, and the scarier one isn't on the field.)**

*Outcome:* The Warden is defeated but left alive. He is humiliated, not martyred. This sets him up to be punished/demoted by the system later, reinforcing the "disposable asset" theme.

#### EVT_W1_05: The Escape

After the Warden falls, Zeke arrives. He physically left his booth. He's shaking, out of breath, terrified. "I hacked the pod! I spliced Jed's Ghost Signal Cell into the core. It's the only way it clears the rail!"

They jump in. The pod launches up the cargo rail. The console reads: **AUTO-NAV: UNKNOWN SIGNAL LOCK.** Zeke hammers the override. Nothing. The Chime is pulling them somewhere.

The Planetary Shield blooms ahead — solid outside the authorized corridor. Impact. Systems fail. The pod spirals toward Sector 9.

Fade to black.

*Zeke's turn:* He didn't just delete a file — he left the building. He burned his career, his safety, his entire identity in under a day. He's not a clerk anymore. He's a loose end.

*Reward:* Zeke joins the party. Source Art: Signal Jammer (Zeke) — tutorialized in World 2.

## V. The Interlude: The Crash

The pod clears the rail with the Chime humming in its core. The nav hard-locks onto a signal in Sector 9 — Orion's Chime, calling from twenty years ago. Zeke hammers the override. Nothing works.

The Planetary Shield isn't a wall. It's a Phase Curtain. Outside the authorized corridor, it's effectively solid. The pod clips the curtain edge and takes catastrophic damage. Systems fail one by one. The pod spirals through the upper canopy and plunges into the Wilds.

*Emotional tone:* Panic collapsing into silence. The roar of re-entry, then the crunch of canopy, then stillness. The first breath of real air Nova has ever tasted.

*Zeke's guilt:* He spliced the Chime in. He can't override the nav. He blames himself for the crash — and he's partially right. This seeds the Zeke-vs-Nova tension that opens World 2.

*What it sets up:*
- The Chime's signal → Orion's Sanctuary → World 2.
- The Planetary Shield → the Lens heist → World 3.
- Nova's uncontrolled Source flare → Orion's training → World 2.
- Zeke's regret → his "useless in the wild" arc → World 2.

## VI. Character States in World 1

### Nova

- **Lie active:** "I'm just a rat in the walls. Survival is enough."
- **State:** Reactive, scrappy, avoidant. She doesn't want trouble — she wants quota.
- **Flaw in action:** Her hustle (rigging the cutter) is what gets her flagged for recycling and sent to Sector 4. Her greatest survival skill is her greatest liability. She solves problems by cheating, and the system punishes cheaters.
- **Growth seed:** Jed's sacrifice plants a question she can't answer yet: why would someone choose to die for another person? Her survival logic has no framework for self-sacrifice.
- **SQ03 seed:** Helping the trapped workers during "Heavy Lifting" is her first act of caring without a payout. It's small, almost accidental. But it's the beginning.
- **End state:** Running. Grieving. Confused. She has power she didn't ask for and a dead father figure she can't bury.

### Zeke

- **Lie active:** "I'm a coward. Following the rules keeps me safe."
- **State:** Complicit, anxious, hiding behind procedure.
- **Flaw in action:** He's been processing recycling orders for years without looking at the people. EVT_W1_04 is the first time he sees a face behind the file number. His efficiency was violence.
- **Growth seed:** Deleting Nova's flag (MQ02) was a reflex — panic, not courage. Leaving his booth to help at the Launch Bay (MQ05) was a choice. By the end of World 1, he's done one impulsive good thing and one deliberate good thing. He doesn't know which one defines him yet.
- **End state:** Terrified. He just destroyed his entire life in a day. He's clinging to the pod controls because it's the only console left.

### Jed

- **Role:** The sacrifice that starts the story. He is not a plot device — he's the person who taught Nova everything she knows and nothing she needs to learn next.
- **His tension with Nova:** Jed wants Nova to survive by keeping her head down. Nova wants to survive by pushing harder. They love each other and disagree about everything that matters. His last words don't validate her approach — "Find the sky" isn't "you were right." It's "go somewhere I can't follow."
- **His choice:** Jed doesn't sacrifice himself because he's brave. He does it because he knows Nova won't stop running long enough to survive unless someone makes her run in the right direction. The Chime is his real gift — not power, but a heading.
- **Legacy:** His wrench. His workshop. The way Nova swears when she's frustrated (she picked it up from him). He haunts the rest of the game not as a ghost but as a standard: "what would Jed do?" The answer is always something stupid and kind.

### The Warden

- **Role:** The system's fist. Not a thinker — an enforcer.
- **Identity:** A careerist in power armor. He doesn't enjoy hurting people, but he doesn't mind it either. He follows orders because following orders is his job, and his job is his identity. He is what Zeke could have become with twenty more years of not looking at the names.
- **Vale dynamic:** The PA interruptions (EVT_W1_02, EVT_W1_05) visibly annoy him. Vale treats the Warden like a blunt instrument — and the Warden knows it but can't argue with a voice that outranks him. This is the first hint of the Dominion power structure cracking from within.

### Vale (Offscreen — PA Relay Voice)

- **Presence:** Two PA interruptions. Calm. Measured. "Cruelty is inefficient."
- **Function:** The player meets the Warden as the villain and Vale as a correction. On first play, Vale sounds like a moderating force — almost reasonable. On replay, you realize he wasn't saying "don't kill her." He was saying "don't waste material." He's worse than the Warden because he's right about the efficiency.
- **Voice identity:** Sterile blue overlay when he speaks (contrasting the Warden's red). Synthetic calm. No anger, no urgency. The voice of someone who has already decided what everything is worth.

### Gh0st (Offscreen)

- **Status:** Deployed by Thorne to hunt the "Anomaly" — the Source spike from EVT_W1_01.
- **Foreshadowing option:** A single environmental detail in Sector 4 — a set of boot prints that are too precise, too evenly spaced, heading toward the Ancient Chamber from a different direction. Nova sees them but doesn't know what they mean. Gh0st was already tracking the signal before she found the Relic. (This pays off in World 2 when Gh0st reveals he's been tracking her.)

## VII. The Architect Layer (World 1 Reveal)

**From the Lore Bible Reveal Ladder:**
> "The Echo Relic speaks in non-Dominion language. The chamber geometry is older than the colony. Dominion didn't invent this — they found it."

### How It Manifests

- **The Ancient Chamber** is architecturally distinct from everything else in the mine. The walls aren't carved — they're grown. The angles follow a logic that isn't human. Where the mine is rust and bolts, this chamber is smooth, resonant, and uncomfortably precise.
- **The Tuning Fork** doesn't beep or click like Dominion tech. It hums at a frequency that pre-dates human language. The SYNC overlay uses symbols Nova (and the player) have never seen.
- **Dominion slapped warning stripes and quarantine tape onto Architect surfaces.** Yellow-and-black tape over alien geometry. This is the visual metaphor for the entire game: Dominion takes things it doesn't understand and brands them.
- **The "magnetic interference"** that Bogs sent Nova to fix was the Tuning Fork's passive resonance. Dominion's sensors read it as noise. It was a signal they couldn't decode.

### What the Player Should Feel

Curiosity, not comprehension. World 1 doesn't explain the Architects. It shows the player that this mine — this whole planet — is built on top of something older and stranger than the corporation that runs it. The questions are the point: Who built this? Why is it here? Why does it respond to her?

The answers come later. World 1 only asks the questions.

## X. Environmental Storytelling Beats

These are non-interactive or lightly-interactive details that build the world without dialogue or quest mechanics. They're for room descriptions and background art.

### Hub 1: Homestead

- **The Memorial Wall (The Pit):** A metal plate bolted to the wall near the bunks. Names laser-etched. Some old, some recent. No flowers, no ceremony. "Retired Assets" is the official heading. People walk past it every day.
- **The Quota Board (Jed's Workshop area):** A public leaderboard showing daily output by worker ID. Nova's ID is in the middle. The top producers get extra scrip. The bottom producers get "reviewed."
- **Dominion Motivational Posters:** "Your Shift Is Your Contribution." "Efficiency Is Safety." "Report Anomalies — Your Silence Is Compliance." They're everywhere, faded, peeling at the corners.
- **The Coffee Machine (The Pit):** Broken. Has been broken for weeks. Someone taped a sign to it: "Maintenance Request #47. Status: Denied." It's the colony in miniature.

### Hub 2: Logistics Sector

- **The Processing Window:** Where Zeke works. A row of identical glass booths. Each one has a console with a "CONFIRM / DENY" interface. The chairs are ergonomic. The lighting is clinical. It looks like a bank. It processes death.
- **The Deep Elevator Warning Signs:** "NO UNAUTHORIZED DESCENT BEYOND LEVEL 3." "BIOLOGICAL HAZARD: WEAR ISSUED RESPIRATOR." "DOMINION IS NOT LIABLE FOR INJURIES SUSTAINED BELOW LEVEL 5." The deeper you go, the more warnings there are — and the more they sound like waivers, not cautions.
- **The Ancient Chamber Transition:** The moment the mine walls stop looking human-made. The shift is gradual: bolts give way to seamless joins, rough stone becomes smooth geometry, and the air gets colder. The last Dominion object is a quarantine barrier with a handwritten note: "Equipment malfunctioning beyond this point. Sensor readings unreliable."

## XI. Week Prep Notes

### For Tuesday: Enemy Design

World 1's enemy roster reflects the colony ecosystem: mining wildlife + Dominion security + automation.

**Current roster (from existing docs — 8 total, within the 6-8 Content Standard):**
- **Common (5):** Rock-Borer (beast/melee/burrow), Security Drone (machine/ranged/alert), Dominion Guard (human/ranged/cover), Riot Guard (human/shield — Guard Break teacher), Fume Bat (beast/swarm/blind)
- **Elite (2):** Heavy Automaton (machine/tank/claw), Overseer Drone (machine/support/buff+heal)
- **Boss (1):** The Warden (human/tank/shield/summoner)

**Narrative roles to develop Tuesday:**
- *Rock-Borers* are wildlife — the mine disturbed their habitat. They're not evil, just territorial. Possible environmental storytelling: Dominion doesn't exterminate them because the miners do it for free.
- *Security Drones and Guards* are the system's enforcers — faceless, replaceable, doing their shift. Guards use cover and flashbangs. Drones alert other enemies if not killed fast.
- *Riot Guards* exist specifically to teach Guard Break/Stagger. Their shields are overkill for a mining colony — these are riot-suppression units, meaning the colony has had riots before.
- *Fume Bats* are the mine's ambient threat — fast, erratic, attack in swarms, apply Blind. AoE (Pulse Grenade) is the answer.
- *Heavy Automaton* is the first real "wall" enemy — high HP, heavy armor, slow. Stagger/Guard Break opens damage windows.
- *Overseer Drone* is the first "support" enemy — buffs and heals other machines. Kill it first or the fight drags.
- *The Warden* combines all lessons: Shield (Guard Break), Drones (AoE), Zones (positioning), and Source (Blast Wave).

**Teaching priority for Tuesday design:** Physical / Shock / Burn elements. Delay Freeze and Source-heavy interactions. Delay Jammed and Marked (Zeke joins too late in W1).

### For Wednesday: Gameplay Gimmick

World 1's unique mechanical identity should grow from the setting. Directions to explore:

- **Jury-Rigging:** Nova modifies equipment from found scrap. The illegal cutter mod is a story beat — could be the tutorial for a broader "improvised modification" system or a simplified crafting-in-the-field mechanic.
- **Environmental Hazards:** Gas pockets, collapsing tunnels, electrical surges — the mine is dangerous outside of combat. Hazards could be obstacles, combat modifiers, or puzzle elements.
- **The Echo Pulse:** After syncing with the Tuning Fork, brief Source "pulses" could reveal hidden paths, interactable objects, or enemy weaknesses — a stripped-down precursor to the Lens's True Sight that only works in short bursts.
- **Note:** The gimmick should feel like a natural extension of Nova's character (clever, resourceful, working with scraps) rather than a generic dungeon mechanic.

## XII. Resolved Design Decisions

1. **Prologue Placement:** Cold Open (Pre-Title). Short (<60s), vibe-focused, establishing the Source mystery before Nova wakes up in normalcy.
2. **SQ03 Gating:** Soft Gate with Fail-Safe. Heavily signposted by Jed/Bogs. If the player skips it, MQ03's first Riot Shield encounter will include a forced tutorial popup/voice line for the mechanic.
3. **Point of No Return (PONR):** Hard PONR at the Deep Elevator (start of MQ03). Explicit warning: "Entering Deep Mine will lock down surface access." This keeps the pacing tight and avoids the "Homestead is burning but let's do sidequests" dissonance.
4. **Gh0st's Boot Prints:** Included. Subtle environmental detail in Sector 4.
5. **The Warden's Fate:** Survives. Defeated and left behind to be "recycled" or demoted by the Dominion later. Reinforces the theme that the system eats its own.
